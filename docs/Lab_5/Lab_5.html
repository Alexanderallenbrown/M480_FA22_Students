Implementing a Controller for the "Motor-Spring-Load" Configuration¶

What do you think will happen if we add an inertia to the motor system attached by a rigid shaft the way your inertia modules are setup now:

The brass disk will certainly increase mass the moment of inertia. But will that increase the number of INDEPENDENT energy storing elements? Because of the rigid connection, the brass mass moment of inertia will certainly have the SAME angular velocity as the motor inertia. That means the number of independent energy storing elements will NOT increase and the order of the system model need not change. It will still be reasonably represented by a 1st order differential equation.

HOWEVER, if a spring connects the motor and the brass load, the motor angular velocity and the brass inertia angular velocity will NOT be the same. This will mean there are 3 independent energy storying elements: Motor inertia, Brass inertia, and the spring. It means that you should expect that it will require at least a 3rd order to capture the dynamics!

Since we don't have experience with 3rd order systems, we'll use our knowledge of superposition to allow us to use transfer function forms we are familiar with. Specifically, the 3rd order response can be modeled as the combination the response of a 1st order system and a 2nd order system. Lets do that together.

Controller Design: Motor-Spring-Load¶

Since you don't yet have tools to do the design of a proportional controller for a higher order system, we'll use model order reduction to proceed. "Model order reduction" is the act of representing a higher-order system as a lower-order one. In this case, you will use only the "First order portion" of your plant model in order to design your controller. For the purposes of controller design, you will ignore the "second order part" entirely during the design process.
